# An Efficient Newton Algorithm for Nonnegative Matrix Factorization with the Kullback-Leibler Divergence 

Damien Lesens ∗ J´ er´ emy E. Cohen † Bora U¸ car ‡

Abstract 

Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) is a fundamental tool in unsupervised learning, which approximates a nonnegative matrix by the product of two low-rank nonnegative factors. The Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence is best suited to measure the data to model discrepancy when the decomposed data sample follows a Poisson distribution, which is the case for count datasets such as term-document matrices or images. Most KL-NMF algorithms in the literature minimize a separable majorant of the loss to find their next iterate. We argue that this method has reached its limits and propose to use instead the second-order Taylor expansion of the loss, leading to a Newton-type method. We minimize this non-separable surrogate by proposing a generalization of the well-known HALS algorithm. This yields an efficient KL-NMF algorithm which provably converges and which competes favorably with state-of-the-art algorithms on a large variety of datasets.

Alternating algorithms solving (NMF-KL) try to decrease the (NNKL) loss for each column of the updated factor. In a seminal paper, Lee and Sung [14] proposed the Multiplicative Updates (MU) algorithm. It minimizes a tight separable upper-bound of the (NNKL) loss to find the next iterate, and it is still the state of the art on many datasets [15]. The MU algorithm is an instance of the more general Majorization-Minimization (MM) framework [16]. Another MM type algorithm for (NNKL) was proposed by Bauschke et al. [17], and then used to solve (NMF-KL) [15]. Our contribution in this manuscript is two-fold. We first give some theoretical results on Majorization-Minimization algorithms [14, 17]. We show that the MU algorithm is optimal amongst algorithms that use a global separable majorant of the loss as a surrogate. These results motivate us to propose a novel algorithm that utilizes a non-separable surrogate. The most obvious candidate for a non-separable surrogate is the second-order Taylor expansion of the loss. In the case of unconstrained optimization, Newton methods minimize exactly this approximation and are very popular in second-order optimization [18]. Some works on the periphery of the NMF literature [19, 20, 21] have tried to apply these tools to the (NNKL) problem. There are however several issues concerning Newton steps in this context. First, it is not clear how to handle the non-negativity constraints. Projected Newton steps [21] or active-set like algorithms [19, 20] do not provide any guarantees in general. Second, computing a Newton descent direction can be costly, even in the unconstrained case. This is a critical issue if one wishes to compete with fast first-order algorithms. Last but not least, it is not clear which step size to choose in the absence of Lipschitz smoothness hypotheses. One can always resort to backtracking, but it can be a very resource intensive process. The work of Tran-Dinh et al. [22] provides an answer on how to handle non-negativity con-straints, as well as which step size to choose. They propose a second-order optimization framework for the constraint optimization of self-concordant functions. This class of functions is not a subset of Lipschitz smooth functions and contains the (NNKL) loss. Our work is in some sense a special-ization of their framework to (NMF-KL). However the specialization is not straightforward. Our 2main contribution is to show that minimizing the second-order Taylor expansion under nonnega-tivity constraints can be done efficiently with a generalization of the HALS algorithm [23]. Our algorithm has complexity O(M N R 2) per iteration, where M × N is the size of the data matrix 

V and R is the target rank. All the algorithms that use a separable surrogate have a cost per iteration of O(M N R ). Our experimental results show that our O(M N R 2) method is competitive with state-of-the-art O(M N R ) algorithm. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. First, we give in Section 2 an overview of existing algorithms for (NMF-KL), with a focus on algorithms closely related to our method. Namely, we introduce Majorization-Minimization algorithms as well as some second-order algorithms. In Section 3.1 we give some results that motivate us to depart from those paradigms and propose in the rest of Section 3 our novel algorithm. Finally, we explain in Section 4 the implementation details of our method and present experimental results.

Their algorithm is akin to an active set algorithm for NNLS and incorporates some Newton steps. In a similar fashion, Hansen et al. # 3 KL-HALS: The proposed algorithm based on HALS 

Here, we present the proposed algorithm for (NMF-KL). We first study two known upper-bounds used in MM algorithms (Section 3.1) and we show the optimality of the MU upper-bound. Based on this finding, we propose to use a quadratic approximation of the loss function (Section 3.2). The quadratic surrogate is minimized using a generalization of the HALS algorithm (Section 3.3). Alternating the minimization for the two factors yields the proposed algorithm, which we call KL-HALS (Section 3.4). Finally, by using the right step size our algorithm is proven to converge (Section 3.5).

3.5 Convergence results 

In this section we give some convergence guarantees on Algorithm 2. We show that our algorithm is a realization of the framework of Tran-Dinh et al. [22]. Their results guarantee that with the right step size at Line 14 of Algorithm 1 each factor update will decrease the (NMF-KL) loss. Let us start with a definition. 

Definition 3.1 (Self-concordant functions [38]) . A convex function f : Rn → R is said to be self-concordant with parameter M ≥ 0 if |φ′′′ (t)| ≤ M φ ′′ (t)3/2, where φ(t) := f (s + tv) for all

The next iterate is then a point found by taking a step αk along the Newton direction, in other words xk+1 := xk + αkdk.Tran-Dinh et al. prove that their algorithm follows an usual pattern in Newton-like algorithms, namely that there are two modes of convergence. If the current iterate is close to the optimal, we are in the basin of quadratic attraction: full Newton steps with αk = 1 guarantee convergence with a quadratic rate. When outside of the basin of quadratic attraction, we have to perform damped 9Newton steps with an αk less than 1. The proximity to an optimal point is measured through the quantity λk =

The Proximal-Newton Method performs full Newton steps ( αk = 1) when λk is less than a constant σ, and damped Newton steps (αk < 1) otherwise (we refer to the original work [22] for more details). We apply the factor update algorithm only a fixed number of times and we do not wait until convergence to a minimum. Hence, we cannot use the convergence rate results. Instead, we utilize the following proposition by Tran-Dinh et al. which shows that the loss (NNKL) decreases when damped Newton steps αk = 1 /(1 + λk) are used. 

Proposition 3.3 (parts of Theorem 6 [22]) . If αk = 1 /(1 + λk) ∈ (0 , 1] , then the damped Newton step xk+1 := xk + αkdk satisfies

where ω : R → R+ is defined as ω(t) := t − ln(1 + t).

A proof is given in Section E for self-containment. We propose two versions of Algorithm 1: 

• KL-HALS-descent: This version performs damped Newton steps with αk = 1 /(1 + λk). It requires the computation of the Newton decrement λk. The cost of computing this quantity for all rows is O(N R 2) ( O(R2) repeated N times). This cost is negligible compared to computing the descent direction and the tensor T. The use of the step size 1 /(1 + λk) in Algorithm 1 ensures that the loss decreases at each factor update. The loss is positive and decreases at each factor update so it converges to a limit point. 

• KL-HALS: This version always performs full Newton steps with αk = 1, even outside of the basin of quadratic convergence. It is a heuristic which makes the assumption that we will make more progress with larger step sizes, even though we do not have any descent guarantee. We will see in Section 4 that this assumption is verified: on all tested instances KL-HALS converges faster than KL-HALS-descent. KL-HALS-descent is proven to converge, but we do not have any guarantee on the point the algorithm converges to (e.g. local minimum, stationary point). Razaviyayn et al. [39] proposed the Block Successive Convex Approximation (BSCA) framework, which ensures the converge to a stationary point for factor update algorithms similar to KL-HALS. However, we could only make KL-HALS a true realization of BSCA by making some strong hypothesis on the problem. We thus leave the discussion on the links between KL-HALS and the BSCA framework to the supplementary material in Section F. Another aspect that can help second-order algorithms to converge in practice is a good initial point. We will discuss in Section 4.3 how scaling the rows and columns of the initial model is a very efficient way to start close to the basin of attraction. 

# 4 Experimental results 

In this Section we investigate the experimental performance of our algorithms with respect to other state-of-art algorithms on a large collection of datasets. We first specify some implementation details in Sections 4.1 to 4.3. We then list the algorithms we studied in Section 4.4. Finally, we present experimental results for real-life datasets (Section 4.6) and synthetic datasets (Section 4.7). 10 4.1 Choice of hyper-parameters 

In Algorithm 2 the value of hyper-parameters I (the number of iterations of the proposed GHALS algorithm), and D (the number of times a factor is updated before switching to the other one) needs to be specified. We find empirically that D = 1 is the best choice: when applying Algorithm 2 multiple times on the same factor the loss decrease is not worth the additional computing time. In other words, we make the most progress with the first update of a factor and we are better of switching to the other one after that. For the GHALS parameter I, we find that taking I = 5 is enough, that is after cycling 5 times through all ranks we have found a minimizer of the second-order Taylor expansion.

These quantities also appear in our KL-HALS algorithm (see Algorithm 1). For large and sparse matrices, computing the whole W H matrix, which has M × N entries, is prohibitive. It is the case for example for document datasets [15], where large-scale experiments cannot be performed if a normal implementation is used. We overcome forming the product W H by only computing the entries of W H that correspond to non-zeros in V , whose number (that we note nnz( V )) can be orders of magnitude smaller than M N . This yields a sparse representation of VW H that can be handled efficiently. This way, our code only manipulates sparse matrices and the complexity of algorithms goes, for example, from O(M N R ) for dense matrices, to O(nnz( V )R) for sparse matrices. This allows us to run an extensive evaluation of almost all state-of-the-art algorithm on a collection of large sparse matrices (see Section 4.6.3). We used the scipy library [40] and more specifically the scipy.sparse module to handle sparse matrices and sparse linear algebra. The only algorithms that we could not adapt to handle sparse matrices are the ones that use duality, namely FPA [36] and ADMM [37]. These algorithms require the manipulation of a dual variable of size M × N that cannot be assumed to be sparse. In consequence, we have excluded these algorithms from our evaluation on sparse datasets.

12 4.5 General comments on experimental results 

Overall our KL-HALS algorithm exhibits very good performance on most of the datasets we tested. It is amongst the bests if not the best algorithm, except on the MAPS and Verb dataset. For these datasets in particular we ran algorithms with increasing ranks, and the convergence of KL-HALS started to get slower for R around 50. KL-HALS-descent is consistently slower than KL-HALS. On a large portion of datasets it even struggles to make sufficient progress. This behavior is also observed for the SN algorithm which uses the same self-concordance properties. This suggest that the descent guarantees are an useful tool in theory but they do not translate to fast algorithms in practice. KL-HALS also consistently beats the CCD algorithm. This clearly indicates that taking into account the full second-order information is better than only using the second-order information with respect to single variables. On the datasets we tested, the FPA algorithm is either amongst the worst algorithm or does not converge at all. We note that this algorithms does not come with any descent nor convergence guarantee (see Hien and Gillis [15] for a discussion). We confirm the observations of previous studies [15, 31] by observing that MU, AmSOM and AMUSOM are state-of-the-art algorithms that exhibit fast convergence on all datasets.

Figure 6: Median value of the KL loss on full-rank synthetic datasets: left - 200 ×200, right -500 ×500. 18 5 Conclusion 

In this work, we present a novel algorithm for (NMF-KL) that utilizes the second-order development of the loss. We are the first work on (NMF-KL) proposing an algorithm that minimizes efficiently this non-separable surrogate under nonnegativity constraints. We invoke some results on self-concordant functions to show that there exists an easily computable step-size that makes the proposed algorithm converge. The large scale experiments we conducted show that our method out-performs state-of-the-art algorithms on most datasets. We believe that exploring non-separable surrogate is essential as there is no more margin for progress on separable upper-bounds: we prove that the global separable majorant used by the MU algorithm is the tightest possible. We also prove on the way the sub-optimality of the MU Burg algorithm. A promising research direction is to explore other kinds of non-separable surrogates, as this work shows they can lead to high-quality iterates. We observed in experimental results that the additional time invested to compute those iterates is worthwhile, as the decrease in loss is tangible. Also, some progress could be done on reducing the cost inferred by the computation and the minimization of the quadratic surrogate in our algorithm. Approximations of the Hessian are used in quasi-Newton methods to reduce the cost of iterates. We anticipate that similar approximation techniques could be used to make KL-HALS more competitive with first-order algorithms on datasets with high rank. We observed in our experiments that performing full Newton steps yields better results than shorter steps that are guaranteed to decrease the loss. Recent results on extrapolated updates for the NMF problem [51, 52] could motivate the use of steps even greater than one to accelerate convergence. It would also be interesting to explore whether the self-concordance descent guarantees could be strengthened in the case of (NNKL), by either extending the size of the basin of quadratic convergence or by proving that there exists larger descending steps.

# F The Block Successive Convex Approximation (BSCA) algorithm 

In this section, we slightly modify the NMF-KL problem by ensuring that all entries of the factors are greater than a fixed ϵ > 0. That is, we consider the modified problem min   

> W≥ϵ, H≥ϵ

DKL (V ∥W H ), (NMF-KL-ϵ)with ϵ > 0 very small, usually taken to be machine precision. This perturbation of the problem is discussed in [15]. It is common in the literature as it avoids the zero-locking phenomena [9], that is the factor entries being stuck at zero and enable to change due to the multiplicative nature of the update algorithm. It also makes studying the problem easier as the objective function is now continuous and continuously differentiable on the whole search space. This is the main reason 28 for which we restrict ourselves to this perturbation in this Section.
