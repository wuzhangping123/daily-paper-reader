IMPLICIT REGULARIZATION IN PERTURBED DEEP MATRIX FACTORIZATION: SPECTRAL CONDITIONS AND STABILITY 

JINGZHE WANG ∗ AND HUNG-HSU CHOU †

Abstract. This paper studies the stability of low-rank implicit regularization in perturbed deep matrix factorization, where the target matrix is corrupted by a noise matrix. We first derive sufficient spectral conditions under which gradient descent exhibits a low-rank phase in the noiseless setting. These conditions show how the target spectrum, initialization, and step size jointly determine the existence of a nonempty low-rank interval. We then analyze the perturbed gradient descent dynamics, proving convergence guarantees and quantifying how the perturbation affects iteration complexity and eigenvalue recovery. Finally, we show that the low-rank phase persists under perturbation, with explicit dependence on the perturbation size. Numerical experiments support the theoretical findings. 

Key words. implicit regularization, deep matrix factorization, gradient descent, low-rank ap-proximation, spectral stability, nonconvex optimization

1. Introduction. Deep learning has achieved remarkable success across a wide range of applications, yet its highly overparameterized models and nonconvex training objectives remain challenging to understand theoretically. In particular, it is often unclear why gradient-based methods select solutions that generalize well among many possible minimizers. This has motivated the study of implicit regularization, which refers to the structural bias induced by the optimization algorithm, even in the ab-sence of an explicit regularizer. Since linear networks retain several key properties of nonlinear deep models while remaining sufficiently simple to allow explicit and rigor-ous analysis, a line of research has focused on the implicit regularization of gradient descent based optimization in linear networks [2, 5, 7, 8, 9, 11, 13, 15, 17, 19]. In this setting, training an overparameterized linear network can be viewed as solving a deep matrix factorization optimization problem. This viewpoint makes it possible to study how initialization, step size, and the spectral structure of the ground-truth matrix influence the implicit regularization behavior of gradient descent. The work [4] provides a non-asymptotic analysis of gradient descent for the deep matrix factorization problem (2.3). Their result shows that the gradient descent dynamics (2.5) prefer low-rank solutions under suitable assumptions. Informally, for a prescribed rank L, the gradient descent iterates exhibit a low-rank phase: during a time interval specified by the spectral properties of the ground-truth matrix cW ,the effective rank of the current iterate remains close to that of the best rank-L

approximation of cW . We refer to this interval as the low-rank interval [ T0, T 1], as defined in (2.8) and (2.9). In this paper, we study the perturbed deep matrix factorization (4.1). Instead of optimizing against the exact ground-truth matrix cW , we consider the perturbed target fW = cW + E, where E is a noise matrix. Such perturbations arise, for in-stance, when the ground-truth matrix is corrupted by measurement noise, or when random noise is added as part of a privacy-preserving procedure. Motivated by these settings, including differentially private matrix approximation [6, 14], we study how additive spectral perturbations affect the implicit regularization behavior of deep ma-

1.1. Main Contributions. This paper rigorously quantifies how additive noise affects implicit regularization of (4.1) under the gradient descent dynamics (4.2). Our goal is to show that the low-rank phase persists in the presence of noise: there is a nonempty perturbed time interval [ eT0, eT1], as defined in (5.1), over which the effective rank of the iterates remains close to that of the rank-L approximation of the noiseless target, up to an explicit error depending on the perturbation size ∥E∥. We model E as a Gaussian noise matrix. Standard concentration bounds imply that ∥E∥ is bounded with high probability; our deterministic stability analysis is therefore carried out on this high-probability event, with bounds expressed in terms of the perturbation size. We summarize our contributions as follows. 

Existence of low-rank intervals in the noiseless problem. We revisit the noiseless result in [4, Theorem 3.5] and provide a general spectral analysis in Theorem 3.6, showing how the spectrum of the ground-truth matrix cW , the step size, and the initialization of gradient descent jointly govern the existence of nonempty low-rank intervals ( T0 < T 1). These conditions depend explicitly on spectral gaps and help explain when low-rank behavior can be expected. Concrete illustrations are provided in Figures 1a and 1b. 

Spectral stability under perturbation. Building on the existence of such low-rank intervals, we provide a stability analysis of implicit regularization for the perturbed gradient descent dynamics (4.2). Our proof extends the method of [4] by combin-ing it with matrix perturbation theory. Under the assumptions that the noiseless ground-truth matrix cW is positive semidefinite and the perturbation matrix E is symmetric, we analyze the dynamics at the level of individual eigenvalues. We first establish convergence of gradient descent dynamics (4.2) for the perturbed deep ma-trix factorization problem (4.1). Theorem 4.3 and Theorem 4.4 further quantify how the perturbation size affects the iteration complexity and the recovery of nonnegative eigenvalues, respectively. 

Persistence of low-rank implicit regularization. After establishing convergence, we prove our main stability results for implicit regularization. Theorem 5.1 shows that the perturbed dynamics preserve the low-rank phase, with an explicit error depending on the perturbation size. Theorem 5.2 quantifies how the perturbation shifts the endpoints of the low-rank interval from [ T0, T 1] to [ eT0, eT1], while Theorem 5.3 bounds the resulting low-rank approximation error. Together, these results establish the stability of implicit regularization under the perturbed gradient descent dynamics. 

Numerical validation. We provide numerical experiments to validate our theo-retical findings. The empirical results (Figure 1) illustrate the proposed spectral conditions and confirm the stability of implicit regularization (Figure 2) in perturbed deep matrix factorization. 

1.2. Related Work. Deep linear networks provide a tractable setting for study-ing optimization dynamics and implicit regularization in overparameterized models. The work of Saxe et al. [16] showed that, under suitable alignment assumptions, the gradient-flow dynamics of deep linear networks can decompose into modes gov-erned by spectral quantities. This perspective has motivated a large literature on 

> 2

implicit bias and implicit regularization in matrix factorization and linear networks [2, 7, 9, 8, 15, 19, 17, 11, 13, 5]. Much of this literature focuses on analytically convenient regimes, such as gradi-ent flow, infinitesimal initialization, or asymptotic training time. These settings reveal how factorized parametrizations can favor low-complexity, and often low-rank, solu-tions. By contrast, finite-step gradient descent with finite initialization requires more quantitative control. Chou et al. [4] gave a non-asymptotic analysis of this setting and identified spectral conditions under which gradient descent exhibits a transient low-rank phase. However, these conditions do not by themselves give a transparent characterization of when the predicted low-rank interval is nonempty. Our work extends this line of analysis in two directions. We first derive explicit spectral, initialization, and step-size conditions ensuring a nonempty low-rank interval in the noiseless problem. We then prove that this low-rank implicit regularization is stable under additive perturbations of the ground-truth matrix, with quantitative bounds on the shifted interval and the resulting approximation error.

6. Conclusion. This paper analyzed the stability of low-rank implicit regular-ization in perturbed deep matrix factorization. We first revisited the noiseless setting and derived spectral conditions ensuring the existence of a nonempty low-rank inter-val. These conditions make explicit how the target spectrum, initialization, and step size govern the emergence of low-rank behavior along the gradient descent trajectory. We then studied the perturbed problem, where the target matrix is corrupted by additive noise. By analyzing the perturbed gradient descent dynamics at the ei-genvalue level, we established convergence guarantees, quantified the effect of the perturbation size on iteration complexity, and characterized the recovery of nonneg-ative eigenvalues. Building on these results, we proved that the low-rank phase is stable under controlled perturbations: the effective rank remains close to that of the rank-L approximation of the noiseless target over a perturbed low-rank interval, with explicit dependence on the perturbation size. We further quantified the shift of this interval and the resulting low-rank approximation error. 

19 Our results show that the implicit regularization behavior observed in noiseless deep matrix factorization is robust to bounded perturbations of the target matrix. The numerical experiments support the theoretical predictions and demonstrate the role of spectral structure in determining when this robustness is observable. Future work includes extending the analysis to nonsymmetric or indefinite perturbations, de-riving sharper probabilistic bounds for concrete random noise models, and developing analogous stability results for broader classes of nonlinear networks.
