Abstract 

Image restoration faces a fundamental tradeoff: methods that minimize error produce blurry reconstructions, while those that maximize perceptual quality yield sharp but less faithful images. Existing approaches either commit to a single operating point on this distortion perception (DP) frontier or require paired-data supervision, auxiliary models, or hyperparameter tuning of the sampler to access different points. We show that flow map models, a recent extension of flow matching for few-step sampling that learns an average field, implicitly define a one-parameter family of denoisers that continuously spans the DP frontier. The lookahead parameter t acts as a control knob between the MMSE and perceptual regimes. For Gaussian targets, we prove that varying t exactly recovers the optimal DP frontier; for natural images, we observe similar behavior empirically. Within a Plug-and-Play solver, the same mechanism extends to general inverse problems, where it controls a tradeoff between perceptual alignment and data consistency. Despite the lack of exact optimality guarantees in this setting, a single trained flow map spans the DP tradeoff, matching or exceeding specialized baselines at both extremes. Extensive experiments on CelebA ( 128 × 128 ) and AFHQ ( 256 × 256 )across several linear and nonlinear inverse tasks validate our findings. Code is available in https://github.com/nzilberstein/Flow-map-denoisers 

1 Introduction 

Image restoration is a highly ill-posed inverse problem: a single degraded observation can correspond to a vast set of plausible clean images. Standard regression approaches that minimize mean squared error (MSE) approximate the conditional expectation (Ongie et al., 2020), achieving minimum distortion at the cost of over-smoothed reconstructions that lack fine detail. In contrast, posterior sampling techniques (Kawar et al., 2021) produce realistic-looking solutions at the cost of higher distortion. This inherent compromise is formalized by the distortion-perception (DP) tradeoff (Blau and Michaeli, 2018; Freirich et al., 2021). Because the ideal balance between low distortion and high perceptual quality is strictly user-dependent, designing a single model capable of traversing the DP plane at inference time remains a central challenge in computational imaging. Most existing methods commit to a single operating point in the DP plane , either targeting mini-mum distortion through supervised regression (Dong et al., 2015; Terris et al., 2026), or minimum perception by leveraging recent generative models as priors (Pokle et al., 2024; Chung et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie and Simoncelli, 2021) to draw samples from the posterior. A few recent works based on data-dependent flow models aim to traverse the DP plane (Ohayon et al., 2025; Delbracio and

Milanfar, 2023; Albergo et al., 2024), by explicitly interpolating between measurement-dependent estimators (e.g., the conditional mean) and the clean image. Unlike these approaches, which require external mechanisms, such as interpolation, discretization choices, or retraining, to move along the DP frontier, we show that this tradeoff is already implicitly encoded in the dynamics of flow maps. This raises the question of whether this behavior can be achieved without relying on solver choices, such as increasing the number of discretization steps. In this work, we propose a unified approach based on flow maps (Boffi et al., 2025b,a; Geng et al., 2025; Sabour et al., 2025b), which unifies all operating points along the DP plane within a single model. While prior work primarily uses flow maps to accelerate sampling (Gülle et al., 2026; Sabour et al., 2025a; Spagnoletti et al., 2025), we instead leverage them to traverse the DP frontier . Our key observation is that the lookahead t selects a point along a continuum of estimators—termed average denoisers —implicitly encoded within a single trained flow map, thus acting as a knob to control the characteristics of the restored image. This perspective reveals DP control as an intrinsic property of the learned flow map, eliminating the need for multiple models, auxiliary networks, or heuristic solver choices. This family spans the entire DP frontier, recovering the MMSE estimator at one extreme and approaching posterior sampling at the other. For Gaussian targets, we prove that this family spans the entire DP frontier, recovering the MMSE estimator at one extreme and approaching posterior sampling at the other, tracing the optimal DP curve (Freirich et al., 2021). While this exact theoretical correspondence strictly holds for Gaussian noise problems, we empirically observe similar behavior for natural images. When extending this approach to general, non-Gaussian inverse problems, traversing the lookahead serves as an approximation to the true DP frontier; here, the lookahead controls a tradeoff that combines perceptual alignment with data-consistency effects, rather than exactly reproducing the denoising DP frontier. By embedding these denoisers within a Plug-and-Play (PnP) framework (Venkatakrishnan et al., 2013), we leverage this property to obtain a versatile solver that effectively traverses the DP frontier across a wide range of inverse problems. Fig. 1 illustrates this behavior on a 2D Gaussian mixture model. Overall, our contributions are: • Flow maps as a continuum of DP estimators. We reinterpret flow maps as a family of denoisers indexed by a lookahead parameter t, enabling continuous traversal of the DP plane with a single model. • Exact optimality in the Gaussian case. We show that this family recovers the optimal DP curve for Gaussian targets, establishing a direct link between flow maps and DP theory. • PnP reconstruction with continuous control. We embed these denoisers into a plug-and-play framework, yielding a unified solver that spans the entire DP plane without retraining, and enables high-perceptual reconstructions at a fraction of the cost of posterior sampling methods. • Empirical validation. On CelebA and AFHQ inverse problems (inpainting, motion deblurring, super-resolution, Gaussian deblurring), our method matches or exceeds specialized baselines at both DP endpoints from a single trained model, and uniquely traces a smooth DP curve in between.

2.2 Inverse problems and Plug-and-Play methods 

We consider inverse problems of the form 

y = f (x) + v, (6) where f (·) is a known (possibly non-linear) degradation operator and v is an additive noise, typically Gaussian v ∼ N (0 , σ I). Two main families of methods combine flow or diffusion priors with data-consistency updates (Daras et al., 2024; Chung et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2025): guidance-based methods (Zhang et al., 2024; Pokle et al., 2024; Chung et al., 2023; Song et al., 2022) and plug-and-play (optimization-based) methods (Ben-Hamu et al., 2024; Martin et al., 2025). We focus on the latter and defer a discussion of guidance-based approaches to Appendix A.2. 3Plug-and-Play methods. Plug-and-Play (PnP) algorithms (Venkatakrishnan et al., 2013; Romano et al., 2017) originate from proximal optimization methods for solving inverse problems of the form 

min x g(x) + h(x). In particular, forward–backward splitting (FBS) alternates between a gradient step on the data-fidelity term and a proximal step on the regularizer: 

zk = xk − λt∇g(xk), (7) 

xk+1 = prox λh (zk), (8) where the proximal operator is defined as prox λh (z) := arg min x 12 ∥x − z∥2 + λh (x). Computing 

prox λh corresponds to solving a denoising problem. 

Flow and diffusion models as PnP priors. Recent works instantiate this proximal step with a learned denoiser Dt, yielding the iteration xk+1 = Dt(zk) (Zhu et al., 2023; Martin et al., 2025; Mardani et al., 2024; Zilberstein et al., 2025; Renaud et al., 2024; Laumont et al., 2022, 2023; Hu et al., 2024). This approach leverages implicit priors learned by generative models while retaining the flexibility of PnP for arbitrary forward operators. However, these methods typically optimize distortion, often at the expense of perceptual quality, resulting in over-smoothed reconstructions. In Section 3, we show that replacing the instantaneous denoiser with an average denoiser associated to the flow-map enables continuous control over this tradeoff and improves perceptual quality. 

2.3 Distortion-Perception Tradeoff 

The standard evaluation of image restoration methods relies on average distortion D = E[∆( x, ˆx)] 

(e.g., MSE or LPIPS), which measures the discrepancy between the ground truth x and its estimate

3 Average denoisers: traversing the DP plane 

In this section we define and analyze the average denoiser associated to flow maps and its connection to the DP tradeoff (Section 3.1). 

3.1 Flow maps as denoisers 

Recall we seek a family of estimators parametrized by a single model that interpolates between P ∗

(lowest distortion) and P = 0 (lowest perception). A natural candidate is the denoiser implicitly defined by the flow map (Section 2.1). The same object was independently introduced by Lee et al. (2026) for one-step language modeling on discrete data; here we study it in the continuous setting and show it parametrizes the DP frontier. 4Definition 1 (Average denoiser) Given a flow map with average velocity v(., s, t ), the average denoiser (or two-time denoiser) is: 

Ds,t (x) := x + (1 − s)v(x, s, t ). (10) This definition has the same form as the instantaneous denoiser (3) , but replaces the instantaneous velocity v(., s ) with the average velocity v(., s, t ) over the interval [s, t ]. This modification has an important consequence: the parameter t now controls where Ds,t (x) sits on the DP plane, i.e., varying 

t traces a continuous path between the low-distortion and low-perception extremes. Importantly, both 

s and t are inputs of vθ (x, s, t ), so a single trained model produces a whole family of denoisers with different characteristics, as we show in the following section.

3.3 Beyond the Gaussian case: image denoising experiments 

For general image distributions, the average denoiser no longer admits a closed-form characterization. While exact DP optimality is not expected beyond the Gaussian case, the structural insight underlying Theorem 1 should still hold: as t varies from s to 1, the output of Ds,t is expected to interpolate smoothly between the MMSE estimator (low distortion, low perception quality) and a perceptually-aligned estimator (higher distortion, improved perception). Consequently, we expect (i) smooth, monotonic DP curves parameterized by t, and (ii ) convex trajectories consistent with the optimal DP function (Section 2.3), which we verify empirically below. While this section focuses on image data, we further support this intuition for a 2D mixture of Gaussians (MoG) in Appendix D.3, where the DP curve can be computed explicitly. 

Setup. We train a flow map network vθ (x, s, t ) on CelebA 128 × 128 (Appendix B) and evaluate 

Ds,t (xs) at multiple lookahead values t ∈ [s, 1] , where t = s recovers the MMSE denoiser Ds and 

t = 1 reaches the endpoint of the flow. Since the stochastic interpolant (1) at time s takes the form 

xs = s x1 + (1 − s) ϵ with ϵ ∼ N (0 , I), applying the average denoiser Ds,t to xs corresponds to Gaussian denoising: recovering the clean signal x1 from an observation where the signal is scaled by s and corrupted by noise of standard deviation 1 − s. We parameterize the noise level by 

ξ := 1 − s ∈ (0 , 1) , where larger ξ means heavier noise. 

DP traversal. Fig. 2a shows the empirical DP curves obtained by sweeping the lookahead t from 

s (MMSE) to 1 (full perception) across four noise levels ξ ∈ { 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7}. As expected, the average denoiser traces smooth, monotone curves in the DP plane: increasing t improves perception (lower FID) at the cost of distortion (higher RMSE). The resulting curves exhibit the convex shape predicted by the optimal DP function. Unlike prior methods that provide only discrete or heuristic control, our approach yields a smooth and continuous parameterization of the DP frontier from a single trained model. We also observe that the traversal range increases with the noise level. For ξ = 0 .1, the range is narrow since the MMSE estimator is already close to the data distribution, whereas for ξ = 0 .7, the lookahead spans a wide range of operating points. This is consistent with Freirich et al. (2021), as the perception gap P ∗ grows with noise, allowing a larger tradeoff between distortion and perception. Fig. 30 illustrates this effect: as t increases from s to 1, reconstructions transition from smooth averages to sharp, detailed images.

4 Plug-and-play with average denoisers 

We now embed the average denoiser (10) into the PnP framework from Section 2.2 to solve general inverse problems with continuous DP control. Importantly, the DP interpretation applies at the level of the denoiser Ds,t , while the overall PnP solution reflects its interaction with the forward model. As a result, in general inverse problems, the lookahead controls a tradeoff that combines perceptual alignment with data-consistency effects, rather than exactly reproducing the denoising DP frontier. 

Algorithm. Given a trained flow map vθ (·, s, t ), observations y, and a chosen lookahead t ∈ [s, 1] ,our method alternates between a gradient descent step on the data-fidelity term g(x) = 12 ∥y − f (x)∥2

and a proximal step utilizing the average denoiser. Following (Martin et al., 2025), we include a renoising step prior to applying the denoiser. This reprojects the gradient-updated iterate onto the support of the distribution at noise level s, ensuring that the average denoiser Ds,t processes inputs at its intended operational noise level. The complete procedure is summarized in Algorithm 1. 

Role of the renoising step. The stochastic renoising step is what makes the lookahead a DP knob in the inverse-problem setting. With renoising, Ds,t operates at its intended noise level s, placing it in the regime where Theorem 1 characterizes its behavior, and the injected noise restores the sample diversity required at the perceptual end of the frontier. Without renoising, the iteration reduces to a fixed-point Tikhonov estimator and the lookahead degenerates into a bias-variance knob; we treat this case in Appendix D.4. 

Computational cost and connection to PnP-Flow. Algorithm 1 requires one forward pass through 

vθ per iteration. The lookahead t is merely a conditioning input, adding zero overhead and preserving efficiency over methods requiring ODE backpropagation or trace computations. 

5 Related work 

Consistency models and flow maps for inverse problems. Consistency models (Song et al., 2023b) learn to map any point on a probability flow ODE to its origin, enabling single-step generation. Consistency trajectory models (Kim et al., 2025) and flow maps (Boffi et al., 2025a) generalize this to arbitrary pairs (s, t ) by learning the ODE solution operator. More recent works repurposed these models for controlled generation, either as guided samplers (Sabour et al., 2025a; Spagnoletti et al., 2025; Garber and Tirer, 2025; Holderrieth et al., 2026), as priors (Gülle et al., 2026), or for optimizing the noise initialization (Mammadov et al., 2026). Most recently, and concurrently to this work, Huang et al. (2026) also leverage flow maps for guidance, formulating reward-based generation as an optimal control problem and using the flow map for fast guided sampling. However, all these approaches primarily target accelerated sampling at a fixed operating point and do not provide explicit control over the restoration tradeoff. In contrast, we treat the lookahead t as a continuous parameter, turning a single flow map into a family of estimators that spans the entire distortion–perception (DP) frontier. 

Methods that traverse the DP plane. Recent approaches explore distortion–perception control by learning data-dependent flows between degraded and clean distributions. As a result, DP control is obtained from a single model without paired supervision, auxiliary networks, or retraining, and extends naturally to arbitrary forward operators through Plug-and-Play inference.

7 Discussion and limitations 

We introduced flow map denoisers, a generalization of the standard instantaneous denoiser that enables continuous, zero-shot traversal of the DP tradeoff. By reinterpreting flow maps as a continuum of average denoisers indexed by a lookahead parameter t, we link flow maps to the theory of optimal DP estimators. This traversal is provably optimal for Gaussian targets and empirically validated for natural images. Embedded within a Plug-and-Play (PnP) solver, our approach enables inference-time control over restoration quality across diverse degradations, without retraining, paired data, or auxiliary models. Our approach has several limitations. First, the formal optimality result is restricted to the Gaussian case. While the image experiments in Section 3.3 suggest similar behavior more broadly, a general theoretical characterization remains open. In particular, understanding failure modes is an important direction for future work, as certain degradations can lead to non-monotonic DP behavior, like box inpainting in Fig 3. Second, flow map models are more expensive to train than standard flow matching models and may underperform on pure distortion metrics such as PSNR. Third, while increasing the lookahead parameter consistently moves reconstructions toward the perceptual end of the DP spectrum, the resulting endpoint ( t = 1 ) is not always the best perceptual method. This suggests that the lookahead parameter should primarily be viewed as a mechanism for traversing the DP tradeoff rather than as a way of reaching the optimal perceptual operating point for every restoration task. The perspective in this work opens several directions for future work, borrowing ideas from the denoiser literature (Milanfar and Delbracio, 2025). Average denoisers could be trained directly via denoising objectives, such as SURE-based/consistency losses (Raphan and Simoncelli, 2011; Daras et al., 2025; Tachella et al., 2025). Extending the construction to more general noising processes, such as general restoration priors (Hu et al., 2024; Elata et al., 2025), is an additional natural next step. Lastly, our experiments are limited to pixel-space models (CelebA 128, AFHQ 256); extending the framework to latent-space and text-to-image settings is an important direction.

A.1 Flow Maps 

Consider a probability flow defined by the ordinary differential equation 

˙xt = vt(xt), x0 ∼ p0,

which transports samples from a simple distribution p0 to a target distribution p1. The associated flow map Xs,t : Rd → Rd is defined as the solution operator of this ODE: 

Xs,t (xs) = xt,

i.e., it maps a point at time s to its position at time t along the flow trajectory. In particular, X0,1

directly maps samples from p0 to p1, enabling one-step generation without numerical integration. A convenient parameterization of the flow map is given by 

Xs,t (x) = x + ( t − s) v(x, s, t ),

where vs,t can be interpreted as the average velocity between times s and t. In the limit t → s, this recovers the instantaneous velocity field: 

lim  

> s→t

∂tXs,t (x) = v(x, t ),

which implies that v(x, t, t ) = v(x, t ).

Characterization of the flow map. The flow map can be equivalently characterized in three ways: • Lagrangian form: 

∂tXs,t (x) = vt(Xs,t (x)) ,

which describes evolution along trajectories; • Eulerian form: 

∂sXs,t (x) + ∇Xs,t (x) vs(x) = 0 ,

which expresses conservation along the flow; • Semigroup property: 

Xs,t (x) = Xu,t (Xs,u (x)) ,

which encodes consistency across time intervals. These equivalent formulations provide the basis for learning flow maps in practice (Boffi et al., 2025b). 

Self-distillation framework. Instead of learning the instantaneous velocity field v(., t ) and inte-grating the ODE, flow map models directly learn v(., s, t ) by combining: • the diagonal loss in (2) enforcing v(x, t, t ) ≈ v(x, t ),• an off-diagonal loss enforcing consistency with one of the above three characterizations. Notice that throughout this work we used the LSD loss in (5) , which is the one associated to the Lagrangian condition. This approach, known as self-distillation , allows training a single model that approximates the full flow map Xs,t without requiring a pre-trained teacher model.

B Implementation 

B.1 Training details 

All models are trained unconditionally using the Flow Map objective (Boffi et al., 2025b) with the LSD loss (5). 

Architecture. We use the SongUNet Song et al. (2021) – a UNet with self-attention – with a base channel width of 128 and channel multipliers [1 , 2, 3, 4] , giving four resolution scales. Self-attention is applied at spatial resolutions 16 × 16 and 8 × 8. Each resolution level contains 3 residual blocks, dropout rate 0.1, and a cosine resampling filter [1 , 3, 3, 1] .

Time conditioning. The network is conditioned on two scalars (αr , α t) ∈ [0 , 1] 2 representing the interpolation times of the source and target. Both are encoded with a positional (sinusoidal) embedding . Following Boffi et al. (2025b), the two conditioning scalars are embedded as PE( αt −αr )

and PE( αr ), summed, and passed through a two-layer MLP with SiLU activations to produce a global conditioning vector injected into every residual block via adaptive group normalisation. 

Loss weighting. For the off-diagonal term, we sample s, t uniformly from {(s, t ) : 0 < s < t ≤ 1}

as detailed in Boffi et al. (2025b). We follow Karras et al. (2024) and learn a per-sample uncertainty weight for the LSD loss, we attach a small network that outputs a scalar log-variance log σ2(αr , α t) ∈

R per training example. The network takes the concatenated pair [αr , α t] ∈ RB×2×H×W and generate a scalar used to weight the loss as L ∝ e− log σ2

ℓ + log σ2.

At t = s, both RMSE and FID decrease monoton-ically with N and plateau, consistent with the local-contraction analysis of Theorem 2. At t = 1 ,the perceptual trajectory is non-monotonic: FID drops sharply within the first few tens of iterations, reaches its minimum, and then drifts back up as N continues to grow. The effect is most pronounced for Gaussian deblurring, where the best FID at t = 1 (≈ 11 at N ≈ 20 ) is reached an order of magnitude earlier than the best FID at t = s (≈ 23 .5 at N ≈ 300 ). D (t)

grows with t, reflecting the transition from a contractive MMSE estimator at t = s to a less regular, perceptually-oriented estimator at t = 1 . RMSE remains well-behaved in both cases, indicating that the drift is along the perceptual axis. The practical takeaway is that the two endpoints call for different iteration schemes: the perceptual endpoint ( t = 1 ) is best run for fewer steps than the distortion endpoint ( t = s). Why x⋆(t) is not the MAP/Wiener estimator. One might expect PnP with Ds,t =s (the MMSE denoiser) to recover the MAP estimator xMAP , as in classical PnP with proximal operators. This is not the case. The flow-map denoiser Ds,t (x) = Λ( s, t ) x is not a proximal map: it operates at the intrinsic noise level σs of the interpolant xs, rather than the measurement noise σobs .Matching (40) to xMAP would require c(t)/λ = σ2obs /σ 2

, which is incompatible with the con-traction regime. Consequently, PnP-Flow operates at an effectively higher noise level, yielding 45 over-regularized estimates. As the lookahead t increases, c(t) decreases and x⋆(t) moves toward the Wiener solution, reducing bias. Thus, the lookahead controls a bias–variance trade-off induced by this noise mismatch, rather than traversing the optimal distortion–perception curve. In general, x⋆(t)

Effect of renoising in the Gaussian setting. To isolate the role of the renoising step, we repeat the experiment with the full stochastic PnP iteration. While the deterministic case converges to a single Tikhonov solution, the stochastic iteration produces a distribution of estimates whose spread is largely independent of the step size λ.Across different λ, the sample clouds consistently exhibit meaningful variance and align with the geometry of the data distribution, even when the mean estimate remains over-regularized. This indicates that the renoising step, together with the denoiser, primarily controls the variance of the estimator, while λ governs its mean through the data-consistency update. As the lookahead increases, this variability is progressively amplified, yielding the distortion– perception behavior observed in the experiment. 46
