# Low-degree estimation thresholds in planted hypergraphs and tensor PCA 

Daniel Fu ∗1 and Youngtak Sohn †11Division of Applied Mathematics, Brown University 

Abstract 

A central question in high-dimensional statistics is to understand statistical–computational gaps : regimes in which recovering a hidden signal is information-theoretically possible but conjectured to be computationally intractable. The low-degree framework offers a concrete way to study this gap by restricting attention to estimators that are polynomials of degree at most D in the observed data. In this paper, we study low-degree estimation in planted dense subhypergraph, sparse tensor PCA, and tensor PCA with a general prior. For the planted dense subhypergraph model on n vertices, we identify two regimes depending on whether the planted set is larger or smaller than √n. Above this scale, we identify a sharp threshold for low-degree estimation. Below this scale, we establish hardness in the regimes predicted by prior work, thereby resolving a question of Schramm and Wein (2022) and Sohn and Wein (2025). For sparse tensor PCA, we identify an analogous sharp phase transition. For tensor PCA with a general prior, we prove a low-degree estimation lower bound at the critical signal scale, matching the degree–signal tradeoff suggested by prior work. Our lower bounds apply to degree D = nδ , where n is the dimension and δ > 0 is a constant, and we complement them with corresponding low-degree upper bounds. In addition, for planted dense subhypergraph and sparse tensor PCA above the √n scale, we convert our upper bounds into polynomial-time algorithms that achieve almost exact recovery above the sharp threshold, yielding polynomial-time algorithms succeeding up to this threshold. Our proofs extend the framework of Sohn and Wein (2025) through a conditional variant that yields the correct signal-to-noise ratio in settings where the unconditional approach is insufficient. 

# 1 Introduction 

A fundamental task in high-dimensional statistics is to recover a hidden signal buried in a large, noisy dataset. Alongside the information-theoretic question of determining the weakest signal strength at which recovery is possible by any estimator, the high dimensionality of these problems introduces a 

computational challenge: whether recovery can be achieved by an efficient algorithm of practical runtime. When there is a gap between the signal strength needed for information-theoretic recovery and that needed for computationally efficient recovery, we say there is a statistical–computational gap . Understanding the nature of such gaps is one of the central goals of high-dimensional statistics and average-case complexity. In this work, we study three closely-related models in which a signal is buried in a random tensor or random hypergraph, each exhibiting a statistical–computational gap:

21.1 Low-degree estimation framework 

Each of the three models described above is an average-case problem: the goal is to find an estimator that runs in polynomial time and recovers the signal with high probability when the input 

Y is sampled from the model, rather than an estimator that succeeds uniformly over all inputs. Proving unconditional computational lower bounds for such problems—for instance, showing that no polynomial-time algorithm can estimate θ with probability 1 − o(1) as n → ∞ —appears beyond the reach of current complexity theory, so one seeks evidence for hardness through a variety of restricted computational frameworks and structural barriers. Prominent examples include the sum-of-squares hierarchy [ RSS18 ], the statistical query model [ BBH +21 ], reductions from conjecturally hard problems [ BB20 ], and geometric obstructions based on the overlap gap property [ Gam21 ]. Among these, the low-degree polynomial framework has been successful, with a strong track record of correctly predicting the computational thresholds in average-case inference problems. The low-degree framework was first developed for detection [ HS17 , HKP +17 ], where the central object is the low-degree likelihood ratio [ KWB19 ]. Its estimation analog was introduced by Schramm and Wein [ SW22 ], who defined the low-degree MMSE as the smallest mean-squared error achievable by a polynomial estimator of degree at most D. Suppose that the goal is to estimate a scalar x

from the observed data Y : in planted dense subhypergraph and sparse tensor PCA, x will be the first coordinate of the signal θ1. The degree-D minimum-mean-squared-error (MMSE) is defined by

A useful benchmark is the degree-0 case, where MMSE ≤0 is the error of the trivial estimator 

f (Y ) = E[x], so that MMSE ≤0 = Var (x). Accordingly, MMSE ≤D ≥ (1 − o(1)) MMSE ≤0 says that degree-D polynomials have, asymptotically, no better performance than trivial estimation. This is the form of hardness that we establish, with degrees as large as D = nδ for some constant δ > 0. For the types of average-case problems considered here, degree-O(log n) polynomials are often already rich enough to match the performance of the best currently known polynomial-time algorithms. Consequently, when one can show that polynomials of super-logarithmic degree fail, this is typically viewed as evidence that no polynomial-time algorithm should succeed. In this sense, the term “low-degree” usually refers to degree-O( log n). More generally, the guiding heuristic is that degree-D polynomials correspond to algorithms with runtime n eO(D) where eO(·) suppresses poly-logarithmic factors in n. This heuristic was formalized in work of Hopkins [ Hop18 , Hypothesis 2.1.5, Conjecture 2.2.4] for detection problems, but it is now known not to hold in full generality [ BHJK25 ]. Determining the correct general formulation remains an active open problem; see the recent survey [ Wei25 , Section 6] for a broader discussion of how low-degree lower bounds should be interpreted. Nonetheless, the framework’s predictions have consistently matched the conjectured thresholds for natural high-dimensional models such as planted clique [ SW22 ] and community detection [ SW25 ], and we accordingly view our lower bounds as strong evidence for computational hardness.

For r = 2, the same low-degree phase transition was established by Sohn and Wein [ SW25 ], and matching polynomial-time recovery at the same threshold was achieved by the AMP algorithm of Hajek, Wu, and Xu [ HWX18 ]. r ≥ 3, the threshold was predicted heuristically, via an AMP analysis, by Corinzia et al. For tensor PCA with general prior ( r ≥ 3), the prediction is qualitatively different: it is expected that there is a smooth tradeoff between the degree D and the signal strength for weak recovery, rather than a sharp threshold. For the Rademacher prior, the analogous detection lower bound was proved by Kunisky, Wein, and Bandeira [ KWB19 ], and for the spherical prior, the corresponding estimation lower bound was proved by Kunisky, Moore, and Wein [ KMW24 ] (among other results). Our work treats a general class of independent priors under mild moment assumptions, via a different proof method based on cumulant bound of Schramm and Wein [SW22].

6Discussion The planted dense subhypergraph problem has extensive literature. For r = 2, it reduces to planted dense subgraph model, which has been studied from both statistical and compu-tational perspectives. Statistical limits, both the information-theoretic threshold and performance of the maximum likelihood estimator, are established in [ ACV14 , VAC15 , CX16 , HWX17 ]. On the computational side, hardness has been studied through low-degree polynomial lower bounds for detec-tion [ DMW25 ] and for estimation [ SW22 , SW25 ], and through average-case reductions [ BB20 , BJ23 ]. There are also positive algorithmic results [ BCC +10 , Ame15 , CX16 , Mon15 ]. The special case 

r = 2 , q 1 = 1 is the planted clique problem [ Jer92 , AKS98 , DM15 , FGR +17 , BHK +19 , GZ19 ], a canonical benchmark for average-case hardness and a base for establishing reduction-based hardness in many inference problems [ BR13 , BBH18 , BB20 ]. For r ≥ 3, relatively less is known. The special case q1 = 1 is the hypergraphic planted clique problem; we refer to [ LZ20 ] for a discussion of open problems related to its average-case hardness. For general q1 < 1, statistical limits for weak recovery were determined by Yuan and Shang [YS21]. On the computational side, Luo and Zhang [ LZ22 ] studied the regime ρ ≥ n−1/2 from both directions: on the hardness side [ LZ22 , Theorem 20] rules out low-degree estimation under the condition SNR ≤ n−δ for a fixed δ > 0 as n → ∞ , and on the algorithmic side [ LZ22 , Proposition 1] proves that an aggregated-SVD recovers the planted set {i ∈ [n] : θi = 1 } exactly once SNR ≥ c

for some constant c > 0. 4 Theorem 2.2 improves their result by determining the threshold at constant-level: estimation is low-degree hard for SNR < 1, with a matching upper bound for 

SNR > 1, locating the critical point with constant e/ (r − 2)!. While preparing this paper, we became aware that the same value was predicted by the heuristic AMP analysis of Corinzia, Penna, Szpankowski, and Buhmann [ CPSB22 ] (see the discussion after Theorem 2.5 for sparse tensor PCA). Theorem 2.7 in Section 2.4 lifts the upper bound in part (b) to a polynomial-time algorithm via a color-coding trick [ AYZ95 , HS17 , MWXY24 ], achieving almost-exact recovery, which appears to be the first to attain this sharp threshold for all r ≥ 2. A brute-force search over ℓ-element subsets of [ n] with ℓ = eO(λ−2/(r−1) ) yields a recovery algorithm of θ1 with runtime exp ( eO(λ−2/(r−1) )) (where eO hides the polylogarithmic factors), which is an adaptation of the subexponential time algorithms for the spiked Wigner model [ DKWB23 ] and sparse tensor PCA [ Cd21 ]. This matches our lower bound under the degree-runtime heuristic (Section 1.1). See the discussion following Theorem 2.5 for the analogous matching in sparse tensor PCA. In the regime ρ ≤ n−1/2, the best known algorithm for the planted dense subgraph ( r = 2) is the caterpillar-counting algorithm of Bhaskara et al. [ BCC +10 ], which succeeds for a < bξ . The matching hardness, however, was only partially understood: Schramm and Wein [ SW22 ] established low-degree estimation hardness in the region a > b/ 2, leaving open the gap b/ 2 > a > bξ . Whether estimation hardness extends throughout a > bξ , or whether a better algorithm could succeed up to a < b/ 2, was raised as an open question in [ SW22 ] and reiterated by Sohn and Wein [ SW25 ]. For the corresponding detection task, Dhawan, Mao, and Wein [ DMW25 ] had established hardness throughout a > bξ ; this does not settle the estimation question, however, as low-degree estimation hardness does not formally follow from its detection counterpart and is generally more delicate to establish. In fact, recent work by Tang, Han, and Zhang [ THZ26 ] shows that in a certain tensor cumulant inference problem, there are regimes in which computationally efficient estimation is possible while the detection task is low-degree hard. 2.4 Efficient algorithm for estimation 

Although Theorem 2.2-(b) and Theorem 2.5-(b) establish the existence of degree-Or,ε ( log n) polyno-mials achieving near-perfect correlation with the estimand x = θ1 above the sharp thresholds, a naive evaluation of these polynomials would take nOr,ε (log n) time. The polynomials in our proofs are, however, tree-shaped: each is indexed by a hypertree (see Definition 2.8) on k = Or,ε ( log n) vertices. For such tree-shaped polynomials, the color-coding trick of Alon, Yuster, and Zwick [ AYZ95 ] gives a randomized polynomial-time implementation. Namely, we randomly color the n vertices using k

colors and restrict the sum to colorful hypertrees, i.e., hypertrees whose k vertices receive distinct colors. For a fixed hypertree, this occurs with probability q = k!/k k = n−O(1) . After rescaling by 1/q , the colorful count is an unbiased estimator of the original tree-shaped polynomial. # 3 Proof techniques

For planted dense subhypergraph with ρ ≤ n−1/2, we introduce a conditional version of this method, namely orthogonal expansion with conditioning . 3.2 Orthogonal expansion approach with conditioning 

For the planted dense subhypergraph model with ρ ≤ n−1/2 (i.e. ξ ≤ 1/2), the orthogonal expansion approach in Section 3.1 is insufficient to establish hardness throughout a > bξ . The difficulty is that when a < b/ 2, there exists rare configurations of Y that make cα ≡ E[ϕα(Y )θ1] substantially greater than its typical value whenever the hypergraph α is ‘dense’; that is, has many hyperedges relative to its number of vertices. This in turn inflates the norm of the dual certificate u. A similar phenomenon was observed by Dhawan, Mao, and Wein [ DMW25 ] who considered detection of the planted dense subhypergraph; for this reason, they considered a conditioning approach to the low-degree likelihood ratio. Similar to the approach of [ DMW25 ], we introduce a method for incorporating conditioning into the orthogonal expansion. Let E⋆ be a ‘good’ event, chosen so that E⋆ occurs with high probability, 16 but rules out the rare configurations which cause inflating cα = E[ϕα(Y )θ1] than the typical value of ϕα(Y )θ1.
